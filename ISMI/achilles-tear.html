Understanding the Injury

What Is the Achilles Tendon?

A tendon is a structure that attaches a muscle to bone. The Achilles tendon connects the calf muscles to the back of the heel bone (calcaneus) — making it essential for walking, running, jumping, and nearly every lower-body movement. Achilles pain can stem from several structures in the same area, and sometimes more than one issue is present at the same time.

Achilles Tendon

The large tendon connecting your calf muscles to your heel bone. Overuse or trauma can cause tendonitis, tendinopathy, or a full tear.

Haglund's Deformity

A bony bump on the back of the calcaneus that can rub against the Achilles tendon and cause chronic irritation and pain.

Retrocalcaneal Bursa

A fluid-filled sac located near the tendon's attachment to the heel. When inflamed (bursitis), it can be particularly painful and tender to the touch.

Risk Factors

Common Causes of Achilles Tendon Injury

Achilles tendonitis is usually due to repetitive stress that leads to irritation and inflammation. If left untreated, this can progress to chronic changes in the tendon called tendinopathy. Several factors can make you more susceptible to these injuries.

Tight or Poor-Fitting Footwear

Shoes that are too tight or lack adequate support increase pressure on the tendon and surrounding structures with every step.

Flat Feet or High Arches

Abnormal foot mechanics place uneven loads on the Achilles tendon, increasing stress and the likelihood of irritation over time.

Tight Calf Muscles

Limited flexibility in the calf increases tension through the Achilles, especially during push-off movements like running or climbing stairs.

Sudden Increase in Activity

Ramping up walking, running, or hiking volume too quickly — especially without adequate conditioning — is one of the most common triggers.

Repetitive Overuse

Sports and activities that involve repetitive loading — running, jumping, basketball, tennis — create cumulative stress that can exceed the tendon's tolerance.

Yoga & Flexibility Activities

Certain yoga poses that place the foot in extreme dorsiflexion or apply sustained stretch to the calf have been associated with Achilles tendon irritation.

From tendonitis to tears: When repetitive irritation goes untreated, it can lead to chronic tendinopathy — structural changes in the tendon itself. You can also develop actual tears in the Achilles, either from long-standing tendinopathy or a sudden traumatic injury.
Know the Signs

Symptoms of an Achilles Tendon Injury

Achilles injuries range from gradual overuse conditions to acute traumatic ruptures. Understanding which type of symptoms you're experiencing helps determine the urgency of care.

Gradual / Overuse

Tendonitis & Tendinopathy

Tenderness when touching the tendon
Stiffness with the first few steps of the day
Pain that worsens with activity
Swelling along the tendon
Acute / Traumatic

Possible Achilles Rupture

Sudden, painful "pop" sensation
Rapid swelling and bruising
Difficulty bearing weight or walking
Pain at the back of the lower leg
Traumatic rupture symptoms require evaluation within several days. Surgical outcomes are significantly better with prompt treatment.
Don't Wait

Suspect a Traumatic Achilles Rupture?

If you develop pain, swelling, and bruising after a sensation of popping during physical activity, this could be a sign of a traumatic Achilles rupture. This type of injury often requires surgery, and outcomes are significantly better the sooner appropriate treatment is provided. Don't try to walk it off — seek an evaluation within several days.

Self-Care

Things You Can Try at Home

For acute Achilles tendonitis, early self-care can help reduce pain and inflammation. The sooner these treatments are started, the better — but they are not a substitute for a physician evaluation if symptoms persist.

01

Change Your Footwear

Switch to comfortable, well-fitted shoes with adequate heel support. Avoid tight footwear that compresses the tendon.

02

Gentle Calf Stretching

Slow, controlled calf stretches can help reduce tension on the Achilles. Avoid aggressive or ballistic stretching during an acute flare.

03

Ice & Rest

Applying ice to the affected area and reducing high-impact activities can help calm acute inflammation and relieve pain.

04

Short-Term NSAIDs

Anti-inflammatory medications (like ibuprofen) may provide short-term relief for acute tendonitis. Long-term NSAID use is not recommended for chronic tendinopathy.

If your symptoms don't improve, or if you suspect a traumatic rupture, you should seek evaluation by a physician promptly. Long-term use of NSAIDs for chronic Achilles tendinopathy may actually delay or inhibit the healing process.
Clinical Care

What a Physician Can Offer

It's important to make sure the correct problem is being treated. Achilles pain can involve the tendon, the bursa, or a bony deformity — and sometimes all three. A physician will begin by identifying the root cause through imaging before recommending treatment.

X-Ray Used to identify bone abnormalities like Haglund's deformity or calcification near the tendon attachment.
Diagnostic Ultrasound Real-time imaging that allows the physician to visualize tendon structure, detect tears, and assess the bursa — performed in-office at ISMI.
MRI Ordered when ultrasound is inconclusive or when a partial or complete tear is suspected that can't be fully visualized otherwise.

Most Achilles tendon issues can be treated without surgery. Surgical intervention is only recommended when conservative treatment fails or in the case of a traumatic rupture.
